Areas of focus

Five domains. Each tested under pressure.

01

AI Governance

Flagship

AI governance is not a technical question. It is an accountability question — and most organisations are structurally unprepared for it. The EU AI Act does not introduce new obligations so much as it reveals existing gaps: in accountability chains, in documentation, in the line of responsibility from technical teams to board level.

Work in this area covers EU AI Act readiness assessment, high-risk AI system classification, governance model design, accountability and RACI framework development, and board-level oversight mechanisms. Built and implemented at an organisation operating AI systems across every major global market — including biometric processing, automated decision-making, and content moderation.

The approach: AI governance designed to hold under enforcement scrutiny — not to satisfy an audit checklist.

02

Global Privacy Architecture

Privacy frameworks operate in tension: rigorous enough to satisfy regulators across multiple jurisdictions, yet operational enough to function inside a real organisation with competing priorities. Getting that balance right requires more than legal knowledge — it requires understanding how governance structures behave under operational pressure.

Frameworks designed and implemented under GDPR, CCPA, PIPL with CAC regulatory approval in China, POPIA in South Africa, and the Privacy Act across Australia and New Zealand. Each implementation required jurisdictional adaptation, local authority engagement, and integration with existing risk and compliance functions.

Privacy governance built to be defensible, not decorative — designed from the outset to withstand a supervisory audit, a data breach, or a board-level inquiry.

03

Authority Engagement & Enforcement

Regulatory engagement at this level is not a communication exercise. It is a strategic discipline — requiring understanding of how supervisory authorities think, what they look for, and how to present an organisation's position credibly under scrutiny.

Direct supervisory authority engagement across NAIH, CNIL, ICO, AEPD, IDPC Malta, CAC, and multiple other European data protection authorities. Engagement contexts have included routine audits, enforcement proceedings, breach notifications, and proactive regulatory consultations.

In addition: strategic direction of data protection litigation across EU, UK, and US jurisdictions — managing external counsel, structuring legal strategy, and positioning the organisation across proceedings where regulatory risk intersected with legal exposure.

04

Crisis & Breach Management

Data breaches do not follow governance frameworks. They expose the gaps in them. The quality of a privacy function is most visible not in its documentation, but in how it performs when something goes wrong — under time pressure, with incomplete information, with executives and regulators both demanding answers simultaneously.

Breach response experience spanning multiple sectors and jurisdictions: notification strategy, regulatory communication, internal containment, third-party coordination, and post-incident governance remediation. Crisis management in contexts where reputational exposure, enforcement risk, and litigation potential were all in play at once.

Structured response under pressure — reducing regulatory exposure while preserving organisational credibility and positioning for any subsequent enforcement review.

05

Board-Level Risk Advisory

Privacy and AI governance reach their strategic value when they inform board-level decision-making — not as compliance updates, but as structured risk intelligence that shapes how an organisation allocates resources, designs products, and enters new markets.

Executive reporting to CEO and Executive Chairman level. Translation of regulatory complexity into decision-relevant risk framing: what is the actual exposure, what are the options, what does each option cost against what it protects.

Converting regulatory complexity into executive clarity — so that the board can govern what they cannot see, and be accountable for what they do not fully understand.
