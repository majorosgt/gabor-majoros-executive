Perspective

Privacy & AI Governance is not
a desk job.

A view from inside the pressure — not above it.

Privacy and AI governance, done properly, is a daily discipline of risk, judgment, and accountability. It does not happen in policy documents. It happens in the moments when something is going wrong, and someone has to decide what to do next.

This is what that work looks like from the inside.

"The quality of a governance function is not visible in its documentation. It becomes visible when the system is under pressure — and someone has to hold it together."

The central reality

On the nature of the work

This field is not about frameworks. It is about what happens when frameworks meet reality — and reality wins.

An open enforcement proceeding. An active data breach. A board meeting tomorrow. A regulator requesting documentation that does not yet exist. These are not edge cases. They are the operating conditions.

Governance without operational depth is decoration. It looks correct on paper, satisfies the audit, and collapses under the first real test. The difference between a governance function that holds and one that doesn't is not in the policies — it is in the judgment of the person running it, and the experience they bring to the moment when the policies stop being sufficient.

That judgment cannot be acquired from certifications alone. It is built through exposure — through having been in the room when the decisions were made, having faced the authority on the other side of the table, and having managed the consequences when things did not go as planned.

Why this demands more

Experience alone is not enough.
Neither is compliance expertise.

The regulatory landscape does not stand still. New frameworks emerge. Existing ones are enforced in unexpected ways. Technologies outpace the rules written to govern them.

Navigating this requires something beyond knowledge of what the law currently says. It requires the creativity to anticipate where regulatory expectation is moving — and the strategic flexibility to position an organisation ahead of that movement, rather than behind it.

It also requires the willingness to operate in exposed positions. Not from behind a layer of external counsel and legal disclaimers, but as the person who stands in front of a supervisory authority, answers to the board, and takes responsibility for the position the organisation has taken. The role is inherently visible. The accountability is direct.

The combination — deep experience, regulatory creativity, and the composure to operate under genuine pressure — is what distinguishes a governance function that shapes an organisation's risk profile from one that merely documents it.

Why this matters here

This is the pedigree the work demands.

Legal foundation built across eight years of international advisory practice. Operational credibility earned across enforcement-facing in-house mandates in banking, aviation, and global operations at scale.

Thirty-plus GDPR implementations. Direct authority engagement across ten jurisdictions. Breach response, litigation strategy, AI governance — not as isolated projects, but as concurrent responsibilities managed under the same executive accountability.

This is not a profile assembled from credentials. It is a record of having done the work — in the conditions the work actually demands — and having built governance functions that held when they needed to.

That is the foundation. That is why the next mandate should be at Chief Privacy Officer level — not as an aspiration, but as the logical continuation of a career built exactly for it.
